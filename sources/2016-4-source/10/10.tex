{\bf 1. Introduction.} The goal of the current paper is to
introduce a modified approach to the problem of local asymptotic
stability of cycles in special non-linear systems and to make a
comparison. The standard approach to this problem is well outlined
in [1, 2] which is a further development of the method describe by
\"O. Morg\"ul [3].

We will distinguish between the systems with no time delays
\begin{equation}\label{dsi}
x_{k+1}= f(x_k), \;  f\in \mathbb R^m,\; k=0,1,...,
\end{equation}
and with time delays of the form
\begin{equation}\label{nsc}
x_{k+1}=a_1f(x_k)+a_2f(x_{k-T})+...+a_Nf(x_{k-(N-1)T}), \;  f\in
\mathbb R^m,\; k=0,1,..., T\in\mathbb Z_+ .
\end{equation}
Note that the systems \eqref{nsc} appear in the problems of
localization of unknown periodic orbits and control of chaos.

In scalar case the standard approach to investigate stability with
no delays is to construct a new map that has points of the cycle
as equilibriums and then linearize about the equilibrims. Our
approach is to construct an auxiliary system that has the cycle
elements as coordinates of the equilibrium vector. So, instead of
studying cycles of a scalar system we study the equilibrium of a
vector system.

In vector case the standard approach produces a characteristic
equation as a result of applying  the chain rule to the new
system, while our approach produces the same equation as a
consequence of the non-degenerate property for the auxiliary
system linearized around equilibrium.

At this level the advantages of our approach are not visible, but
later on they will be more transparent.

For a system with time delays  the standard approach leads to a
system with dimension that increases with delays. The
characteristic polynomials of such systems appear in a standard
form that makes impossible analysis on Schur stability, thus
useless. Our approach leads to a very well structured system where
the characteristic polynomial appears factorized with each factor
in a special form that enables stability analysis. Mo-\linebreak
reover, this new form (see \eqref{chep} on p.~\pageref{chep}) is
very compact and is convenient in applications.

{\bf 2. Main results.} {\it A. Standard approach.} Let us consider
the scalar case of the system \eqref{dsi}. Based on a cycle
$\{\eta_1,...,\eta_T\} $ let us construct a new dynamical system
\begin{equation}\label{ds}
x_{k+T}=f^{(T)}(x_k),
\end{equation}
which iterates $T$ times the initial map. Therefore
$$
 f^{(T)}(x)=f(f^{(T-1)}(x)),..., f^{(1)}(x)=f(x).
$$
Now, let $F(x)=f^{(T)}(x)$ (see the figure below) and denote
$x_0=y_0,\; x_T=y_1,\; \ldots,\; x_{kT}=y_k,\;\ldots \;.$ Then the
system \eqref{ds} has changed to
\begin{equation}\label{dsy}
y_{k+1}=F(y_k).
\end{equation}

\noindent In fact for any $j=0,...,T-1$ we can write
$$
x_j=y_0, \;~ x_{j+T}=y_1, ..., x_{j+kT}=y_k,...
$$
and obtain the same dynamical system \eqref{dsy}.

Note that the system \eqref{dsy} has $T$ equilibriums
$\eta_1,...,\eta_T.$  Then the cycle $\{\eta_1,...,\eta_T\}$ of
the system \eqref{dsi} is asymptotically locally stable if and
only if all equilibriums  $\eta_1,...,\eta_T$ of the system
\eqref{dsy} are asymptotically locally stable.

The multiplier of every equilibrium $\eta_j$ of the system
\eqref{dsy} is  $F^\prime(\eta_j),\; j=1,...,T.$ By the chain rule
$$
F^\prime(\eta_1)=f^\prime(f(f...f(\eta_1)))\cdot
f^\prime(f...f(\eta_1)) \cdots f^\prime(\eta_1)=\mu_T\cdots\mu_1
$$
where $\mu_j=f^\prime(\eta_j).$  It is clear that
$F^\prime(\eta_j)=\mu_T\cdots\mu_1, \; j=1,..,T$. The quantity
$\mu_T\cdots\mu_1$ is called a multiplier for the cycle
$\{\eta_1,...,\eta_T\}.$ The condition of the local asymptotic
stability is $|\mu_T\cdots\mu_1|<1.$



{\it B. Alternative approach}. We suggest considering a system

\begin{equation}\label{ns}
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
x_{k+1}&=&f(x_k),\\
x_{k+2}&=&f(x_{k+1}),\\
.......&...&............... \\
x_{k+T}&=&f(x_{k+T-1}).\\
\end{array}\right.
\end{equation}

\noindent Let

\[
z_1=\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_1\\
x_2\\
\vdots\\
x_T\\
\end{array}\right),\quad
z_2=\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{T+1}\\
x_{T+2}\\
\vdots\\
x_{2T}\\
\end{array}\right)
,\cdots, z_k=\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{(k-1)T+1}\\
x_{(k-2)T+2}\\
\vdots\\
x_{kT}\\
\end{array}\right),\;
z_{k+1}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{kT+1}\\
x_{kT+2}\\
\vdots\\
x_{(k+1)T}\\
\end{array}\right),... \;.
\]

\noindent A very simple but a very important observation is that
\eqref{ns} can be written in the following form:

\begin{equation}\label{nsa}
z_{k+1}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
f(x_{kT})\\
f(x_{kT+1})\\
\vdots\\
f(x_{kT+T-1})\\
\end{array}\right).
\end{equation}

Let us note that \eqref{nsa} is a dynamical system $z_{k+1}=\tilde
F(z_k)$ where

\begin{equation}\label{nsb}
\tilde F(z_k)=\left(\begin{array}{c}
f^{(1)}(x_{kT})\\
f^{(2)}(x_{kT})\\
\vdots\\
f^{(T)}(x_{kT})\\
\end{array}\right).
\end{equation}

\noindent Our novelty is that the asymptotically local stability
of the cycle  $\{\eta_1,...,\eta_T\}$ of the system \eqref{dsi}
corresponds the asymptotically local stability of the equilibrium

\begin{equation}\label{e}
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_1\\
\vdots\\
\eta_T\\
\end{array}\right)
\end{equation}
of the system \eqref{ns}.

In the standard approach the cycle corresponds to $T$ equilibriums
of the scalar dynamical systems. In our approach this cycle
corresponds to one equilibrium of the vector system. So far these
two approaches seems absolutely equivalent. The difference will be
visible when we start to consider the systems with time delays,
especially when the number of the used delays is much larger
compared to the size of the cycle.

To compute multipliers we have to linearize the system \eqref{ns}
in the neighborhood of the equilibrium \eqref{e}
$$
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\delta_k^{(1)}\\
\vdots\\
\delta_k^{(T)}\\
\end{array}\right)=z_{k}-
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_{1}\\
\vdots\\
\eta_{T}\\
\end{array}\right)
$$
and extract the linear part. One can linearize either system
\eqref{nsa} or system \eqref{nsb}. It turns out that it is more
convenient to linearize system \eqref{nsa} because in that case we
can avoid dealing with a superposition of the maps. The linearized
system has the form
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\delta^{(1)}_{k+1}&=&f^\prime(\eta_T)\delta_k^{(T)},\\
\delta^{(2)}_{k+1}&=&f^\prime(\eta_1)\delta_{k+1}^{(1)},\\
.......&...&........................\\
\delta^{(T)}_{k+1}&=&f^\prime(\eta_{T-1})\delta_{k+1}^{(T-1)}.\\
\end{array}\right.
\]
Letting $\delta_{k}^{(j)}=\lambda^ks_j$ ($s_j\not= 0$) we obtain
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\lambda s_1&=&\mu_T s_T,\\
\lambda s_2&=&\mu_1\lambda s_1,\\
.......&...&........\\
\lambda s_T&=&\mu_{T-1}\lambda s_{T-1}\\
\end{array}\right.
\Rightarrow \left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\lambda s_1&=&\mu_T\cdots\mu_1 s_1,\\
\lambda s_2&=&\mu_1\cdots\mu_2 s_2,\\
.......&...&........\\
\lambda s_T&=&\mu_{T-1}\cdots\mu_T s_T.\\
\end{array}\right.
\]
Thus, we come up with the same representation for the multipliers
$\lambda=\mu_T\cdots\mu_1.$

{\it C. Vector case}. It is amazing that there is no major changes
in the vector case.

Namely, in standard approach the Jacobi matrices
$M_j=f^\prime(\eta_j)$ came up and the multipliers are the roots
of the characteristic polynomial
$$
|\lambda I-M_T\cdots M_1|=0,
$$
while in our case
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\lambda \bar s_1&=&M_T\cdots M_1 \bar s_1,\\
\lambda \bar s_2&=&M_1\cdots M_2 \bar s_2,\\
.......&...&........................\\
\lambda \bar s_T&=&M_{T-1}\cdots M_T \bar s_T,\\
\end{array}\right.
\]
where $\bar s_j$ are non-zero vectors. Since the eigenvalues of
the product of square matrices $AB$ and $BA$ are the same (c.~f.
[4, Ex.~9, p.~55]) we come out with the characteristic equation
$$
|\lambda I-M_T\cdots M_1|=0.
$$
In this case the eigenvalues of the matrix $M_T\cdots M_1$ are
called multipliers of the cycle. If all multipliers in absolute
values are less then one then the cycle is asymptotically stable.


{\it D. Systems with time delay}. Let us consider a system with
time delay  in a general form
\begin{equation}\label{dela}
x_{k+1}=f(x_k,x_{k-1},...,x_{k-\tau}),\qquad f\in \mathbb R^m,
\;\; \tau\in \mathbb Z_+.
\end{equation}
And let us study a local stability of the cycle
$\{\eta_1,...,\eta_T\}$ \; where $\eta_j\in \mathbb R^m.$ In other
words for all $k\ge\tau+1$ the following equations are valid:
$$
\eta_{(k+1)\mod T}=f(\eta_{k\mod T}, \eta_{(k-1)\mod
T},...,\eta_{(k-\tau)\mod T}).
$$
There abusing notation we assume that $T\mod T=T.$

{\it Standard.} An auxiliary system with respect to the vector of
size $m(\tau+1)$ is
$$
z_{k}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
z_k^{(1)}\\
z_k^{(2)}\\
\vdots\\
z_k^{(\tau+1)}\\
\end{array}\right) =
\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{k-\tau}\\
x_{k-\tau+1}\\
\vdots\\
x_{k}\\
\end{array}\right).
$$
Then
$$
z_{k+1}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
z_{k+1}^{(1)}\\
z_{k+1}^{(2)}\\
\vdots\\
z_{k+1}^{(\tau+1)}\\
\end{array}\right) =
\left(\begin{array}{c}
z_{k}^{(2)}\\
z_{k}^{(3)}\\
\vdots\\
f(z_{k}^{(\tau+1)}, z_{k}^{(\tau)},...,z_{k}^{(1)})\\
\end{array}\right),
$$
i. e.
$$
z_{k+1}=F(z_k).
$$
Note, that  $F\in \mathbb R^{m(\tau+1)}$ in the above formula and
is different from the one on the page 2.

Further, let $z_{k+T}=F(...F(z_k))$ be the  $T$-times iterated map
$F.$ This map can be written as a system
\begin{equation}\label{delb}
y_{k+1}=\Phi(y_k).
\end{equation}

Let us periodically repeat the elements of the cycle: $
\{\eta_1,\eta_2,...,\eta_T,\eta_1,\eta_2,...,\eta_T,...\}. $ The
first $\tau+1$ elements of this sequence form a vector
$$y^*_1=
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_1\\
\eta_2\\
\vdots\\
\end{array}\right).
$$
In the same way we define the vectors
$$y^*_2=
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_2\\
\eta_3\\
\vdots\\
\end{array}\right), \; ...\; ,\;~
y^*_T= \left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_T\\
\eta_1\\
\vdots\\
\end{array}\right).
$$
It is clear that the vectors $y_1^*,...,y_T^*$ are equilibriums of
the system \eqref{delb}.

Then the cycle $\{\eta_1,...,\eta_T\}$ of the system \eqref{dela}
is asymptotically locally stable if and only if all equilibriums
$y_1^*,...,y_T^*$  of the system \eqref{delb} are asymptotically
locally stable.


For the equilibrium of the system $y^*_1$ of the system
\eqref{delb} the Jacobi matrix is defined by the formula with $T$
factors
\begin{equation}\label{vec}
\Phi^\prime(y_1^*)=F^\prime(y^{(*)}_T)\cdots
F^\prime(y^{(*)}_{1}),
\end{equation}
where the matrix $F^\prime(y_j^*)$ has dimensions $m(\tau+1)\times
m(\tau+1)$ and is equal to
\begin{equation}\label{frob}
F^\prime(y_j^*)= \left(\begin{array}{ccccc}
O&I&O&...&O\\
O&O&I&...&O\\
....\\
O&O&O&...&I\\
Q_1^{(j)}&Q_2^{(j)}&Q_3^{(j)}&...&Q_{\tau+1}^{(j)}\\
\end{array}\right).
\end{equation}
There the matrices $O$ and $I$ are zero and unit matrices
correspondingly and are of the dimensions $m\times m.$ Further,
$$
Q_r^{(j)}=\left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial
z^{(r)}}\right|_{y^*_j},\quad r=1,...,\tau+1,\; \; j=1,...,T,
$$
i. e. the value of the derivative evaluated at the point $y^*_j.$%\\

For all other equilibriums $y^*_j$ the Jacobi matrices
$F^\prime(y^*_j)$ can be computed in the same manner and
$$
\Phi^\prime(y_j^*)=F^\prime(y^{(*)}_{(T+j)\mod T})\cdots
F^\prime(y^{(*)}_{j}),
$$
which can be obtained from \eqref{vec} by a cyclic permutation of
the factors, and therefore the eigenvalues of $\Phi^\prime(y_j^*)$
coincide for all $j=1,...,T$ (c. f. [4]).

If all eigenvalues of the matrix $\Phi^\prime(y_j^*)$ which are
roots of the polynomial
\begin{equation}\label{hpa}
p_1(\lambda)=|\lambda I-\Phi^\prime(y_j^*)|
\end{equation}
are less then one in absolute values then the cycle of the system
\eqref{dela} locally assymptotical stable.


Note that in the scalar case $m=1$ the matrices $F^\prime(y_j)$
are in the Frobenius form. Therefore, the matrix \eqref{frob} is a
generalized form of companion matrix. If system is of special case
below, then the product is manageable and the characteristic
equation was found in scalar case  by means of induction in [1].

{\it  Our approach}. The system \eqref{dela} generates the system
\begin{equation}\label{as}
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
x_{k+1}&=&f(x_k,...,x_{k-\tau}),\\
x_{k+2}&=&f(x_{k+1},...,x_{k-\tau+1}),\\
........&...&....................................\\
x_{n+T}&=&f(x_{k+T-1},...,x_{k-\tau+T-1}).\\
\end{array}\right.
\end{equation}
Note that \eqref{as} is a dynamical system, which might be seeing
by replacing in the right hand side each of
$x_{k+1},...,x_{k+T-1}$ by the functions of
$x_{k-\tau},...,x_{k}.$

If
$$
z_{k}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
z_k^{(1)}\\
z_k^{(2)}\\
\vdots\\
z_k^{(T)}\\
\end{array}\right) =
\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{(k-1)T+1}\\
x_{(k-1)T+2}\\
\vdots\\
x_{kT}\\
\end{array}\right),\quad  z_{k}\in \mathbb R^{Tm},
$$\newpage
\noindent then
\begin{equation}\label{asa}
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
z_{k+1}^{(1)}&=&f(z_{k}^{(T)},z_{k}^{(T-1)},...),\\
z_{k+1}^{(2)}&=&f(z_{k+1}^{(1)},z_{k}^{(T)},...),\\
........&...&..............................\\
z_{k+1}^{(T)}&=&f(z_{k+1}^{(T-1)},z_{k+1}^{(T-2)},...).\\
\end{array}\right.
\end{equation}
The system \eqref{asa} can be written in the form
\begin{equation}\label{asb}
z_{k+1}=\tilde\Phi(z_k,z_{k-1},...),\quad \tilde\Phi\in \mathbb
R^{mT}.
\end{equation}
The system \eqref{asb} has an equilibrium

\begin{equation}\label{asc}
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_1\\
\vdots\\
\eta_T
\end{array}\right),\quad \eta_j\in \mathbb R^m,\; \; j=1,...,T.
\end{equation}

To study the behavior of the solutions to the system  \eqref{asb}
in the neighborhood of the equilibrium \eqref{asc} let us make a
substitution
$$
\delta^{(j)}_{k}=z^{(j)}_{k}-\eta_j,\quad k=0,1,...,\;~ j=1,...,T.
$$
So, instead of linearizing of the system \eqref{asb} one can
linearize the equivalent system \eqref{asa}.

As a result we get
\begin{equation}\label{deltas}
\delta_{k+1}=A_0\delta_{k+1}+A_1\delta_{k}+ ... ,
\end{equation}
where
$$
\delta_k=\left(\begin{array}{c}
\delta_k^{(1)}\\
\vdots\\
\delta_k^{(T)}\\
\end{array}\right)
$$
and $A_j$ are square matrices of the dimension $mT\times mT.$ The
matrix $A_0$ on the main diagonal and above have zero entries.
That means that matrix $E-A_0$ is non-degenerate, therefore the
system \eqref{deltas} is a dynamical system. Its characteristic
equation is
$$
|\lambda I-A_0\lambda-A_1-...|=0.
$$
If all roots of this equation in absolute values are less then
one, then the cycle of the system \eqref{as} is locally
asymptotically stable.

The polynomial  $\lambda^{m(\tau+1)-1}|\lambda
I-A_0\lambda-A_1-...|$ has the same degree, same leading
coefficient and the same roots as polynomial \eqref{hpa} therefore
coinside with \eqref{hpa}.

{\it E. Example}. Let us consider a system \eqref{dela} written in
a coordinate form for $(x_k,y_k)^{\rm T}\in \mathbb R^2,\;
k=0,1,...,$ where T means transposition

\begin{equation}\label{ex1}
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
x_{k+1}&=&f(x_k,y_k,x_{k-1},y_{k-1},x_{k-2},y_{k-2}), \\
y_{k+1}&=&g(x_k,y_k,x_{k-1},y_{k-1},x_{k-2},y_{k-2}). \\
\end{array}\right.
\end{equation}
Let us assume that the system \eqref{ex1} has a cycle
$\bar\eta_1,\bar\eta_2$ of the length 2
\begin{equation}\label{ex2}
\bar\eta_1= \left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_{11}\\
\eta_{12}\\
\end{array}\right),  \qquad
\bar\eta_2= \left(\begin{array}{c}
\eta_{21}\\
\eta_{22}\\
\end{array}\right),
\end{equation}
i.~e.
$$
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\eta_{21}&=&f(\eta_{11},\eta_{12},\eta_{21},\eta_{22},\eta_{11},\eta_{12}), \\
\eta_{22}&=&g(\eta_{11},\eta_{12},\eta_{21},\eta_{22},\eta_{11},\eta_{12}),\\
\end{array}\right.
$$
$$
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\eta_{11}&=&f(\eta_{21},\eta_{22},\eta_{11},\eta_{12},\eta_{21},\eta_{22}), \\
\eta_{12}&=&g(\eta_{21},\eta_{22},\eta_{11},\eta_{12},\eta_{21},\eta_{22}), \\
\end{array}\right.
$$
and $\bar\eta_{1}\not=\bar\eta_{2}.$%\\

Thus, for the system \eqref{ex1} we have $m=2,\tau=2$ and $T=2.$
We came up with the following {problem}: write the characteristic
equation for checking the local asymptotic stability of the cycle
\eqref{ex2}.

{\it Standard approach}. Define the vector of the size
$m(\tau+1)=6$
$$
z_k=\left(\begin{array}{c}

z_k^{(2)} \\
z_k^{(3)} \\
\end{array}\right),
$$
where
$$
z_k^{(1)}=\left(\begin{array}{c}

\end{array}\right),\quad
z_k^{(2)}=\left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{k-1}\\
y_{k-1}\\
\end{array}\right),\quad
z_k^{(3)}=\left(\begin{array}{c}

\end{array}\right).
$$
Then the system \eqref{ex1} is equivalent to the system

\left[\begin{array}{c}

z_{k+1}^{(2)}\\
z_{k+1}^{(3)}\\
\end{array}\right]=
\left[\begin{array}{c}

$$
This system define the map

which Jacobi matrix evaluated at the points

$$
correspondingly are

F^\prime(y^*_\sigma)= \left(\begin{array}{cccccc}

0&0&0&0&1&0\\

\gamma_{\sigma1}&\gamma_{\sigma2}&\gamma_{\sigma3}&\gamma_{\sigma5}&\gamma_{\sigma6}&\gamma_{\sigma2}\\

$$\newpage

$$%\end{equation}
where $I$ is a unit matrix of the dimensions $6\times 6.$

{\it  Alternative approach}. The system \eqref{as} has a form
$$
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
x_{k+1}&=&f(x_k,y_k,x_{k-1},y_{k-1},x_{k-2},y_{k-2}), \\
y_{k+1}&=&g(x_k,y_k,x_{k-1},y_{k-1},x_{k-2},y_{k-2}), \\
x_{k+2}&=&f(x_{k+1},y_{k+1},x_{k},y_{k},x_{k-1},y_{k-1}), \\
y_{k+2}&=&g(x_{k+1},y_{k+1},x_{k},y_{k},x_{k-1},y_{k-1}). \\
\end{array}\right.
$$
Define a vector
$$
z_k= \left(\begin{array}{c}
z_k^{(1)}\\
z_k^{(2)}\\
\end{array}\right),
$$
where
$$
z_k^{(1)}= \left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{2(k-1)+1}\\
y_{2(k-1)+1}\\
\end{array}\right),\;~
z_k^{(2)}= \left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{2k}\\
y_{2k}\\
\end{array}\right),
$$
then
$$
z_{k+1}= \left(\begin{array}{c}
x_{2k+1}\\
y_{2k+1}\\
x_{2k+2}\\
y_{2k+2}\\
\end{array}\right)=
\left(\begin{array}{c}
f(x_{2k},y_{2k},x_{2k-1},y_{2k-1},x_{2k-2},y_{2k-2}) \\
g(x_{2k},y_{2k},x_{2k-1},y_{2k-1},x_{2k-2},y_{2k-2}) \\
f(x_{2k+1},y_{2k+1},x_{2k},y_{2k},x_{2k-1},y_{2k-1}) \\
g(x_{2k+1},y_{2k+1},x_{2k},y_{2k},x_{2k-1},y_{2k-1}) \\
\end{array}\right).
$$
After substituting in the third and the forth equations the values
for $x_{2k+1}$ and $y_{2k+1}$ by their values from the first two
equations we get a system
$$
z_{k+1}=\tilde\Phi(z_k, z_{k-1}).
$$
Making a substitution $\delta_k^{(j)}=\bar\eta_j-z_k^{(j)},~
j=1,2$, we linearize the system. Then \eqref{deltas} takes the
form
$$
\left(\begin{array}{c}
\delta_{k+1}^{(1)}\\
\delta_{k+1}^{(2)}\\
\end{array}\right)=
\left[\begin{array}{c} \left(\begin{array}{cc}

 \end{array}\right)\delta_{k}^{(1)}+
\left(\begin{array}{cc}
 \phi_{15} &\phi_{16}\\
\gamma_{15} &\gamma_{16}\\
 \end{array}\right)\delta_{k}^{(2)}+
\left(\begin{array}{cc}
 \phi_{11} &\phi_{12}\\
\gamma_{11} &\gamma_{12}\\
 \end{array}\right)\delta_{k-1}^{(2)}
\\
\left(\begin{array}{cc}
 \phi_{25} &\phi_{26}\\
\gamma_{25} &\gamma_{26}\\
 \end{array}\right)\delta_{k+1}^{(1)}+
\left(\begin{array}{cc}
 \phi_{23} &\phi_{24}\\
\gamma_{23} &\gamma_{24}\\
 \end{array}\right)\delta_{k}^{(2)}+
\left(\begin{array}{cc}
 \phi_{21} &\phi_{22}\\
\gamma_{21} &\gamma_{22}\\
 \end{array}\right)\delta_{k}^{(1)}
\\
\end{array}\right].
$$
From there we get the characteristic polynomial
$$
p_2(\lambda):=\left| \lambda^2I-\left(\begin{array}{cccc}
0&0&0&0\\ 0&0&0&0\\
\phi_{25} &\phi_{26}&0&0\\
\gamma_{25} &\gamma_{26}&0&0\\
 \end{array}\right)\lambda^2 ~-
\right.
$$
$$
- \left. \left(\begin{array}{cccc}

 \gamma_{13} &\gamma_{14}&\gamma_{15} &\gamma_{16}\\
\phi_{21} &\phi_{22}&  \phi_{23} &\phi_{24}\\

-\left(\begin{array}{cccc}

0&0&\gamma_{11} &\gamma_{12}\\

 \end{array}\right)
\right|.

One can verify that { $p_1(\lambda)$ and

{\bf 3. Special case.} Our approach in contrast to the standard
one is allowed for the systems of the special type \eqref{nsc}
which is
$$
x_{k+1}=a_1f(x_k)+a_2f(x_{k-T})+...+a_Nf(x_{k-(N-1)T}),
$$
immediately write the characteristic polynomial. Then
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
z^{(1)}_{k+1}&=&a_1f(z_k^{(T)})+a_2f(z_{k-1}^{(T)})+...+a_Nf(z_{k-N+1}^{(T)}), \\
z^{(2)}_{k+1}&=&a_1f(z_{k+1}^{(1)})+a_2f(z_{k}^{(1)})+...+a_Nf(z_{k-N+2}^{(1)}), \\
z^{(3)}_{k+1}&=&a_1f(z_{k+1}^{(2)})+a_2f(z_{k}^{(2)})+...+a_Nf(z_{k-N+2}^{(2)}), \\
.......&...&.............................................................\\
z^{(T)}_{k+1}&=&a_1f(z_{k+1}^{(T-1)})+a_2f(z_{k}^{(T-1)})+...+a_Nf(z_{k-N+2}^{(T-1)}). \\
\end{array}\right.
\]
Let us denote Jacobi matrices $f^\prime(\eta_j)$ of the dimension
$m\times m$ by $M_j,~ j=1,...,T.$ Let
$z_k^{(j)}=\eta_j+\delta_k^{(j)}.$ By linearizing we get
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\delta^{(1)}_{k+1}&=&M_T(a_1 \delta_k^{(T)}+a_2\delta_{k+1}^{(T)}+...+a_N\delta_{k-N+1}^{(T)}), \\
\delta^{(2)}_{k+1}&=&M_1(a_1 \delta_{k+1}^{(1)}+a_2\delta_{k+2}^{(1)}+...+a_N\delta_{k-N+1}^{(1)}), \\
\delta^{(3)}_{k+1}&=&M_2(a_1 \delta_{k+1}^{(2)}+a_2\delta_{k+2}^{(2)}+...+a_N\delta_{k-N+1}^{(2)}), \\
.......&...&.............................................................\\
\delta^{(T)}_{k+1}&=&M_{T-1} (a_1 \delta_{k+1}^{(T-1)}+a_2\delta_{k+2}^{(T-1)}+...+a_N\delta_{k-N+1}^{(T-1)}). \\
\end{array}\right.
\]
Letting $\delta_k^{(j)}=\lambda^k\bar s_j$ we obtain ($\bar
s_j\not= 0$)
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\lambda^{k+1}\bar s_1&=&M_T(a_1 \lambda^k+...+a_N\lambda^{k-N+1})\bar s_T, \\
\lambda^{k+1}\bar s_2&=&M_1(a_1 \lambda^{k+1}+...+a_N\lambda^{k-N+2})\bar s_1, \\
.......&...&.............................................\\
\lambda^{k+1}\bar s_T&=&M_{T-1}(a_1 \lambda^{k+1}+...+a_N\lambda^{k-N+2})\bar s_{T-1}. \\
\end{array}\right.
\]
Denote $p(\lambda)=a_1 \lambda^N+...+a_N.$ Then
\[
\left\{\begin{array}{ccl}
\lambda^N\bar s_1&=&M_T \;p(\lambda)\bar s_T, \\
\lambda^{N-1}\bar s_2&=&M_1\; p(\lambda)\bar s_1, \\
.......&...&.......................\\
\lambda^{N-1}\bar s_T&=&M_{T-1}\; p(\lambda)\bar s_{T-1}. \\
\end{array}\right.
\]
Since the eigenvalues of the matrix $M_T \cdots M_1$ are
independent on the cyclic reordering of the product matricies [4],
we have
\begin{equation}\label{che}
|\lambda^{1+(N-1)T}I-M_TM_{T-1}\cdots M_1(p(\lambda))^T|=0.
\end{equation}
Let the eigenvalues of the matrix $M_TM_{T-1}\cdots M_1$ be
$\mu_1,...,\mu_m$. Then changing to the product to the Jordan
canonical form one get from \eqref{che}
\begin{equation}\label{chep}
\prod_{j=1}^m(\lambda^{1+(N-1)T}-\mu_j(p(\lambda))^T)=0.
\end{equation}
In special case and $m=1$ the equivalence of the characteristic
polynomials obtain by the standard and by our approach was
established in [1].

{\bf 4. Conclusion.} Assume that the system
\begin{equation} \label{dsc}
x_{k+1}=f(x_k),\qquad  f: A\to A, \;\;  A\subset \mathbb R^m,
\end{equation}
 has an unstable  $T$-cycle  $(\eta_1,...,\eta_T)$. The cycle multipliers $\mu_1,...,\mu_m$ are zeros of the characteristic polynomial
$$
{\rm det}\left( \mu I-\prod_{j=1}^T f^\prime (\eta_j) \right)=0.
$$
Assume that the multipliers are known only approximately, i. e.
located in a region $M\subset \mathbb C.$

Now, let us close the system by a control
\begin{equation}\label{uT}
u_n=-\sum _{j=1}^{N-1}\varepsilon _{j} \left(f \left(x_{n-jT+T}
\right)-f \left(x_{n-jT} \right)\right), \; |\varepsilon _{j}|<1,
\; j=1,\ldots, N-1.
\end{equation}
The closed-loop system $x_{n+1}=f(x_n) +u_n$ can be written as
\begin{equation}\label{ak}
x_{n+1}=\sum_{k=1}^Na_k f (x_{n-kT+T}),\qquad \sum_{k=1}^Na_k=1,
\end{equation}
where $a_k$ and $\varepsilon _{k}$ are in bijection
$$%\begin{equation}\label{eps}
\varepsilon _{j}=\sum_{k=j+1}^Na_k,\; \; j=1,...,N-1.
$$%\end{equation}
Note that the $T$-cycles of the systems \eqref{dsc} and \eqref{ak}
coincide.

The characteristic equation of the linearized around the cycle
system is
$$
\prod_{j=1}^m\left[\lambda^{T(N-1)+1}-\mu_j\left(\sum_{k=1}^N
a_k\lambda^{N-k}\right)^T\right]=0,\quad \mu_j\in M,\; j=1,...,m.
$$
It is required to choose the gain $\varepsilon_j$ in the control
\eqref{uT} such that

i) $T$-cycle of the system \eqref{ak} to be locally stable;

ii) the depth of the prehistory $T(N-1)$ in the control  \eqref{uT} to be minimal.%\\

Thanks to formula \eqref{chep} the above problem can be treated by
the use of geometric function theory of complex variables. This
theory allows to find necessary metric properties of the
exceptional sets of the polynomial mappings of the unit disc
$$
F:\mathbb D\to \mathbb C,\; F(z)=z\left(\sum_{j=1}^N
a_jz^{j-1}\right)^T.
$$
From there one can find the minimal values of $N$ and the optimal coefficients. The scalar case of $T=1,2$  was treated in [5, 6].%\\
