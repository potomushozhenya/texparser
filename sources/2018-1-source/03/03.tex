\textbf{1. Introduction.} The quasidifferential calculus which was
developed by V.~F.~Demyanov and A.~M.~Rubinov around 1980 (see [1,
2])  looks at the first glance as an exact subdifferential
calculus for pairs of subdifferentials which satisfies the
classical rules of additivity and the Leibniz rules. However, a
closer view shows that the  quasidifferential calculus had
far-reaching consequences on generalized convexity which did lead
to a  fun-\linebreak damental reconsideration of  the algebraic
and geometric properties of the semigroup of bounded closed convex
sets. It created a new theory on minimal representation of the
elements of the Minkowski---R\aa dstr\"om---H\"ormander lattice
which consist of equivalence  classes of pairs of bounded closed
convex sets. For details we refer to the book [1].

We begin our paper with a short description of the basics of
quasidifferential calculus and point out in particular its close
analogy to the classical Fr\'echet differential calculus. Then we
switch to generalized convexity  and continue with the Minkowski
duality. Within this context we discuss in details a  separation
theory for bounded closed convex sets by convex sets [3]  and
present a generalization of the Demyanov difference for closed
bounded convex sets in arbitrary Banach spaces [4].  Here we point
out  in particular the very natural connection between the
algebraic lattice rules and their geometric counterparts for
convex sets. Exemplarly we discuss this point in detail for the
order cancellation law which has  the separation law for convex
sets by sets  as a geometric counterpart and which gives rise to a
new research direction that could be called  {\it geometric
algebra for convex sets}.

At the end of the paper we show that in a locally convex vector
space the generalized  Demyanov difference of two closed bounded
convex sets  gives is a separating set.

\textbf{2. The quasidifferential calculus.}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% %
We  begin with a short survey on the funda-\linebreak mental
concept of quasidifferentiation.

\textbf{Definition 1.} {\rm  Let $X$ be a normed linear space and
$U \subseteq X$ an open set. A function $f: U \rightarrow
\mathbb{R}$ is {\it  quasidifferentiable} at $x_0 \in U$ if $f$ is
continuous at $x_{0}$ and

\rm a) for every $v \in X \setminus \{0\}$ the directional
derivative {\small \[\left. \frac{df}{dv} \right|_{x_0} = \lim_{t
\rightarrow 0+} \frac{f(x_0+t v)-f(x_0)}{t}  ~~ \mbox{\rm
exists;}\]}

\rm b) the mapping\[ \left. Df \right|_{x_0} :
\left.   \begin{array}{lcl}  X  & \longrightarrow& \mathbb{R}\\
 v   &\mapsto & \left. \frac{df}{dv}\right|_{x_0}  \end{array} \right \}\in {\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X) ~~ \mbox{\rm with} \]

$$ {\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X)  =  \{\varphi
=p-q~|~p,q:\mathbb{R}^{n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R},~
\mbox{~sublinear and continuous}\}. $$
%
%
%
There exists an obvious similarity between quasidifferentiation
and  the Fr\'echet differential calculus in finite dimensional
spaces, by  extending the notion of a derivative from a linear
functional to a difference of sublinear functions.  The next
theorem is taken from   [5, theorem~10.2.6].

\textbf{\emph{2.1. The Fr\'echet property.}}

\textbf{Theorem 1.}
 {\it  Let $(X,\Vert \cdot \Vert)$ be a finite-dimensional
normed vector space, $U \subseteq X$ an open subset and $ f:U
\longrightarrow    \mathbb{R}$ a locally Lipschitz function. Then
$f$ is quasidifferentiable at $x_{0} \in U$ if and only if there
exists an element $df{\big |}_{x_{0}} \in  {\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X) $
such that for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a $ \delta >
0,$ such that for all $ h \in U$ with $\Vert h \Vert \le \delta$
and $ x_{0} + h \in U$ the following inequality:
$${\big |} f(x_{0} + h) - f(x_{0}) - df{\big |}_{x_{0}}(h) {\big |}
\le \varepsilon \Vert h \Vert \rm \eqno{\rm{(FP})} $$ holds.}

{\it Proof.}  The sufficiency is obvious. Now let $f$ be a locally
Lipschitz function which is quasidifferentiable. Then we have to
show that the condition  (FP) is satisfied. Assume that this is
not true. Then there exists a locally Lipschitz
quasidifferentiable function, $f:U \rightarrow    \mathbb{R},$
which does not satisfy condition (FP). Hence there exists an
$\varepsilon > 0,$ such that for every $ k \in \mathbb{N}$ there
exists an element $h_{k} \in X \setminus \{0\} $ with $\Vert
h_{k}\Vert \le \frac{1}{k}$ and
$$ {\bigg |} f(x_{0} + h_{k}) - f(x_{0}) -
df{\big |}_{x_{0}}(h_{k}) {\bigg |} \ge \varepsilon \Vert
h_{k}\Vert. $$ Since $f:U \rightarrow    \mathbb{R}$ is locally
Lipschitz, there exists a ball $\mathbb{B}(x_{0},r) \subseteq U$
and a real number $ M > 0, $ such that for all $ x,y \in
\mathbb{B}(x_{0},r)$ we have $ {\big |}f(x) - f(y) {\big |} \le M
\Vert x - y \Vert.  $ Now define: $g_{k} = r \frac{h_{k}}{\Vert
h_{k}\Vert} $ and choose a convergent subsequence, also denoted by
$(g_{k})_{k \in \mathbb{N}},$ with $\lim g_{k} = g$ and $\lim
{\alpha}_{k} = 0,$ where $ {\alpha}_{k} = \frac{\Vert
h_{k}\Vert}{r}. $
Then for all $k \in \mathbb{N}$ we have\\
$$ {\bigg |} f(x_{0} + {\alpha}_{k}g) - f(x_{0}) -  df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g) {\bigg |} =$$
$$= {\bigg |}f(x_{0} +{\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) + f(x_{0} +
{\alpha}_{k}g) - f(x_{0} + {\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) -f(x_{0}) ~-$$
$$ -~ df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g) +  df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) {\bigg |}=$$
$$ = {\bigg |}(f(x_{0}+{\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) -
f(x_{0}) - df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}))~- $$
$$-~((f(x_{0} + {\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - f(x_{0}+{\alpha}_{k}g)) -
( df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g) -  df{\big
|}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k})){\bigg |}\ge$$
$$ \ge {\bigg |} f(x_{0}+{\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - f(x_{0}) - df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}){\bigg |}~-$$
$$-~ {\bigg |}(f(x_{0} +{\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - f(x_{0} + {\alpha}_{k}g_{k}))
-( df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - df{\big
|}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g))  {\bigg |}\ge$$
$$ \ge {\bigg |} f(x_{0}+{\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - f(x_{0}) - df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}){\bigg |}~-$$
$$-~ ({\big |}f(x_{0} +{\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - f(x_{0} + {\alpha}_{k}g){\big |}
+ {\bigg |}  df{\big |}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g_{k}) - df{\big
|}_{x_{0}}({\alpha}_{k}g) {\bigg |}\ge$$
$$ \ge {\varepsilon}_{0}{\alpha}_{k}r -(M{\alpha}_{k}
\Vert g_{k}-g\Vert +L{\alpha}_{k}\Vert g_{k}-f\Vert)=$$
$$ =( {\varepsilon}_{0}r -M\Vert g_{k}-g\Vert -L\Vert g_{k}-
f\Vert){\alpha}_{k}.$$ There exists an index $k_{0} \in
\mathbb{N}$ such that for all $k > k_{0}$
$${\varepsilon}_{0}r -M\Vert g_{k}-g\Vert -L\Vert g_{k}-
f\Vert \ge \frac{{\varepsilon}_{0}r}{2},$$ where $L$ denotes the
Lipschitz constant of $df{\big |}_{x_{0}}.$
 Hence for all $ k \ge k_{0}$ we have\\
$$ {\bigg |} \frac{ f(x_{0}+ {\alpha}_{k}g) -f(x_{0})}{{\alpha}_{k}}
-df{\big |}_{x_{0}} {\bigg |} \geq \frac{{\varepsilon}_{0}r}{2},$$
which implies that the directional derivative of the function $f$
in direction $g$ at the point $ x_{0} \in U$ does not exist. But
this is a contradiction to the assumption,  that $f:U \rightarrow
\mathbb{R}$ is quasidifferentiable at $x_{0} \in U.$ {~}
\hfill\fbox{}

From the Fr\'echet property follows  for quasidifferentiable real
valued functions in finite dimensional spaces immediately:
$$ \left. d(\alpha f  + \beta g) \right|_{x_0}  = \alpha \left. d f \right|_{x_0}  + \beta \left. dg \right|_{x_0}, \quad \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}, $$
$$ \left. d(fg) \right|_{x_0} = f(x_0) \left.  dg \right|_{x_0}  +  g(x_0) \left.  df \right|_{x_0}, $$
$$ \left. d \left( \frac{f}{g} \right) \right|_{x_0}  =  \frac{     g(x_0) \left. d f \right|_{x_0}  -  f(x_0) \left. d g \right|_{x_0 }  }{    {  f(x_0) }^ { 2 }}, \quad  f(x_0) \not=  0. $$

\textbf{\emph{2.2.  A Banach space norm for DCH(X).}} A further
important property of the Fr\'echet differential calculus in a
Banach space $(X, \Vert \cdot \Vert)$  is  the basic fact that the
space of gradients  coincides with the dual space $(X', \Vert
\cdot\Vert')$  and is hence complete in the dual norm $\Vert
\cdot\Vert'$. This property, transformed to the quasidifferential
calculus, would request the existence of a norm for the space
DCH(X), under which  it becomes a Banach space. This can indeed be
satisfied as the following  result   shows, which reflects a
further important analogy between the Fr\'echet    and the
quasidifferential calculus.

\textbf{Theorem 2} ([6, theorem 8.1.26]).  {\it Let $(X,\Vert
\cdot \Vert)$ be a Banach space. Then the space
$$\mbox{\rm DCH}(X) = \{\varphi = p-q~|~ p,q \mbox{\it ~are
sublinear and continuous} \}$$ endowed with the norm $\Vert \cdot
\Vert_{\Delta}$ given by
$$\Vert \varphi \Vert_{\Delta} =
\inf_{p,q \atop \varphi = p-q} ~\left\{\max~\left\{\sup_{\Vert x
\Vert \leq 1} p(x), \sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} q(x)\right\}
\right\},$$ where the infimum is taken over all continuous
sublinear functions $p,q$ such that $\varphi =p-q,$ is a Banach
space.}

{\em Proof.~} First we show that $\Vert \varphi \Vert_{\Delta}$ is
well defined for $\varphi \in \mbox{\rm DCH}$ and that $\Vert
\cdot \Vert_{\Delta}$ is a norm.

Let $\varphi=p-q \in \mbox{\rm DCH}(X)$ be given. From $p(0)=q(0)=
0$ it follows that $\Vert \varphi \Vert_{\Delta} \geq 0.$ Since
$p,q$ are Lipschitz continuous,  $\Vert \varphi \Vert_{\Delta} <+
\infty.$ From the definition of $\Vert \cdot \Vert_{\Delta}$ it
follows that for all $\varphi \in {\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X)$ and $ t \in
\rm \mathbb{R}$ the homogeneity condition  $\Vert t \varphi
\Vert_{\Delta} = \vert t \vert \Vert \varphi \Vert_{\Delta}$
holds. Next we prove the triangle inequality.

Let $ \varepsilon > 0 $ be given and let $p_1,p_2,q_1,q_2$ be
continuous~sublinear functions  with $\varphi_1 =p_1-q_1,\mbox{~}
\varphi_2 =p_2-q_2 \in {\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X)$ and
$$\Vert \varphi_1 \Vert_{\Delta} \leq\max ~\left\{\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
p_1(x), \sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} q_1(x)\right \} \leq \Vert
\varphi_1 \Vert_{\Delta} +\varepsilon,$$
$$\Vert \varphi_2 \Vert_{\Delta} \leq\max ~\left \{\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
p_2(x), \sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} q_2(x)\right \} \leq \Vert
\varphi_2 \Vert_{\Delta}  +\varepsilon.$$
 Now we have {\small
\begin{eqnarray*} \Vert \varphi_1+  \varphi_2 \Vert_{\Delta} &
\leq &  \max \left \{\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} [p_1(x)+p_2(x)],
\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
[q_1(x)+q_2(x)]\right \} \leq\\
& \leq& \max \left \{\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} p_1(x)+
\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} p_2(x), \sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
q_1(x)+\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
q_2(x)\right \}\leq\\
&\leq& \max \left \{\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} p_1(x),
\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} q_1(x)\right \}+ \max\left \{
\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
p_2(x),\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1} q_2(x)\right \}\leq\\
&\leq& \Vert \varphi_1 \Vert_{\Delta} +\Vert  \varphi_2
\Vert_{\Delta} +2\varepsilon.
\end{eqnarray*}}
The triangle inequality follows from the arbitrariness of
$\varepsilon > 0.$ In this way we have proved that ${\mbox{\rm
DCH}}(X)$ is a normed vector space.

Now we show that the space ${\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X)$ is complete. Let
$(\varphi_n)_{n\in \mathbb{N}}$ be a  sequence of elements of
${\mbox{\rm DCH} }(X)$ such that
$$\Vert  \varphi_n \Vert_{\Delta}< {1 \over 2^n}.$$
We show that the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \varphi_n$ is
convergent in the space ${\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X).$ This convergence
implies obviously the completeness of the space ${\mbox{\rm
DCH}}(X).$

Let $\varepsilon$ be an arbitrary positive number and let
$p_n^{\varepsilon},q_n^{\varepsilon}$ be continuous~sublinear
functions  with $\varphi_n =p_n^{\varepsilon}-q_n^{\varepsilon}$
and
$$\Vert \varphi_n \Vert_{\Delta} \leq\max ~\left \{\sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
p_n^{\varepsilon}(x), \sup_{\Vert x \Vert \leq 1}
q_n^{\varepsilon}(x)\right \} \leq \Vert \varphi_n \Vert_{\Delta}
+{\varepsilon \over {2^n}}.$$ It follows from the definition of
the norm $\Vert \cdot \Vert_{\Delta}$ that the series
$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} p_n^{\varepsilon}$ and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}
q_n^{\varepsilon}$ are uniformly convergent on the unit ball of
$X,$  thus they have limits $p^{\varepsilon},q^{\varepsilon}$
which are continuous sublinear functions.

Moreover, the sequences $\left(p^{1\over n}\right)_{n \in
\mathbb{N}} $ and $\left(q^{1\over n}\right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$
have limits $p$ and $q$ which are also continuous sublinear
functions. We write $\psi(x)=p(x)-q(x).$ Clearly, $\psi \in
{\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X).$ It remains to show that $\psi$ is a limit of
the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \varphi_n.$

From the above assumptions and a simple calculation it follows
that there exists an index $m_{\varepsilon}$ such that for all $m
\geq m_{\varepsilon}${~~~}
$$\left\Vert \sum_{n=1}^m \varphi_n-(p^{\varepsilon}-q^{\varepsilon}) \right\Vert_{\Delta} \leq
{2 \varepsilon }.$$

Since $p^{1\over n},~q^{1\over n}$ have the limits $p,~q,$ there
exists an $\widetilde {m}_{\varepsilon}$ such that for $m \geq
\widetilde{m}_{\varepsilon}$ {~~}
$$\left\Vert \sum_{n=1}^m \varphi_n-(p-q)\right\Vert_{\Delta} \leq {2 \varepsilon }.$$
The arbitrariness of $\varepsilon > 0 $ implies that the series
$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \varphi_n$ is norm-convergent in the space
${\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X).$ {~} \hfill\fbox{}

Further metrics for DCH(X) are the Demyanov and the Hausdorff
metric which are studied in detail in [7].

\textbf{\emph{2.3. The approach of V. F. Demyanov and A. M.
Rubinov.}} Different from the above presentation  of the
quasidifferential as a difference  of two sublinear functions, V.
F. Demyanov and A. M. Rubinov used an other approach via the
Minkowski duality. More precisely:

In the case of the quasidifferential calculus we have to deal with
a normed vector space $(X,\Vert \cdot \Vert)$ and we endow the
dual space $X^{'}$ with the weak-$*$-topology $\sigma(X,X^{'}).$
The dual norm of $X$ will be denoted by $\Vert \cdot \Vert^{'}$
and $ \langle  \cdot , \cdot \rangle~:~X^{'}\times X \rightarrow
\mathbb{R} $ stands for the dual pairing.\newpage

Moreover, let us denote by  ${\mathcal K}(X^{'}) $ the set of all
compact convex subsets of the dual space $X'$ endowed with the
weak-$\ast$-topology  $\sigma(X,X^{'}).$ It follows from the
theorem of Alaoglu---Bourbaki that the elements of ${\mathcal
{K}}(X^{'})$ are bounded in the dual norm.

Now L. H\"ormander [8] showed  that  an arbitrary sublinear
function $p: X \rightarrow \rm \mathbb{R}$ is continuous in the
norm topology if and only if its subdifferential at the origin
$$\displaystyle{ \partial p|_{0}~= \{ v \in X^{'}~ |~ \langle v,x \rangle \leq
p(x),~x \in X \}} $$
 is a  compact convex subset of the dual space $X'$  with respect to the  weak-$\ast$-topology  $\sigma(X,X^{'})$
of $X'$ and that $p: X \rightarrow \rm \mathbb{R}$ has the
representation $\displaystyle{p(x) = p_{A}(x) = \max_{a \in
A}~\langle a,x \rangle} $ with $A={\partial p}|_{0} \in {\mathcal
K}(X^{'}).$

To formalize this assignment more precisely, let us consider  the
set ${{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}}$ of all
equivalence class of pairs  $(A,B) \in {\mathcal{K}}^{2}(X').$ For
two  pairs of nonempty compact convex subsets the following
relation $ (A,B) \sim (C,D) \mbox{~} \Longleftrightarrow \mbox{~}
p_A +  p_D~=~p_B +  p_C.$ Two pairs $(A,B)$ and $(C,D)$ which
satisfy this relation are called {\it equivalent}, and
${{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}}$  is the
linear\linebreak space of all classes of equivalent pairs. The
element of ${{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}}$
which contains the pair $(A,B)$  is denoted by $[A,B] \in
{{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}}.$
 In 1966 A. G. Pinsker [9] introduced the following
ordering on ${{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}},$
namely: $[A,B] \preceq [C,D] \mbox{~} \Longleftrightarrow \mbox{~}
p_A~+~
 p_D~\leq~p_B~+~ p_C, $ which is inde-\linebreak pendent of the
special choice of representatives, because of the order
cancellation law.

The space $\left({{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\!
\sim}}}, \preceq \right)$ is called the
Minkowski---R{\aa}dstr\"om---H\"ormander space of classes of pairs
of nonempty compact convex sets. It is a complete vector lattice
and a direct calculation shows that the assignment:
$$\rm DCH (X)  \longrightarrow {{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}} ~~$$
\[ \mbox{\rm with ~} \varphi \mapsto [\varphi]= \{(\partial
p{|}_{0},\partial q{|}_{0})~|~~\mbox{\rm with~} \varphi = p
-q,~p,q~ \mbox{continuous and sublinear}\]
 \normalsize is a lattice isomorphism,
called {\it Minkowski duality} (see [5, theorem 3.4.3]). Note that
it is not a Banach lattice with respect
 to the norm $\Vert  \cdot  \Vert_{\Delta}$   given in theorem  2.

V. F. Demyanov and A. M. Rubinov
 assigned to $\varphi \in
\mbox{\rm DCH}(X)$ the set $$[\varphi] = \{(\partial\!
p{|}_{0},\partial q{|}_{0})~|~~ \mbox{\rm with~~} \varphi = p
-q,~p,q~ \mbox{continuous and sublinear}\}   \in
{{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}} $$ and
considered the set ${{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\!
\sim}}}$ of all equivalence class of pairs  $(A,B) \in
{\mathcal{K}}^{2}(X')$  instead of differences of sublinear
functions. They reformulated condition   b)   of Definition 1 in
the following way:

\rm b$'$). There exist two sets $\underline{\partial} f|_{x_0},~
\overline{\partial} f|_{x_0} \in {\mathcal {K}}(X^{'})$  called
{\it sub- and superdifferential} such that
\[g \mapsto \left.
\frac{df}{dg}\right|_{x_0} = \max_{v \in \underline{\partial}
f|_{x_0}} \langle v,g \rangle + \min_{w \in \overline{\partial}
f|_{x_0}} \langle w,g \rangle =  \max_{v \in \underline{\partial}
f|_{x_0}} \langle v,g \rangle - \max_{w \in -\overline{\partial}
f|_{x_0}} \langle w,g \rangle  \in {\mbox{\rm DCH}}(X).\]

The pair ${{\rm
~D}}{f}\big{|}_{x_{0}}~=~(\underline{\partial}f{\big
|}_{x_{0}},\overline{\partial}f{\big |}_{x_{0}})$ consisting of a
sub- and superdifferential  is called a {\it quasidifferential}
\index{Quasidifferential} of $f$ at $x_0 \in U$ and for the class
of all equivalent pairs holds:
 $$ \left[\underline{\partial} f|_{x_0}     ,-\overline{\partial} f|_{x_0}   ) \right]  \in {{\mathcal{K}}^{2}}{(X')}_{{\big{/}_{\!\! \sim}}}$$
which is an element of the  the Minkowski---R\aa
dstr\"om---H\"ormander lattice.\newpage

In the   Section 3 we one step further towards generalised
convexity and geometric algebra for arbitrary topological vector
spaces (see also [10]).

\textbf{3.  The semigroup of closed bounded convex sets.} For a
Hausdorff topological real vector space $(X,\tau)$ let us denote
by ${\mathcal{A}}(X)$ the set of all nonempty subsets of $X,$ by
${{\mathcal{B}}^{\ast}}(X)$ the set of all nonempty bounded
subsets of $X,$ by ${\mathcal{C}}(X)$ the set of all nonempty
closed convex subsets of $X,$ by ${{\mathcal{B}}}(X) =
{{\mathcal{B}}^{\ast}}(X) \cap {\mathcal{C}}(X)$ the set of all
bounded closed convex sets of $X$ and by ${{\mathcal{K}}(X)}$ the
set of all nonempty compact convex subsets of $X.$ For $A,B \in
{\mathcal{A}}(X)$ the {\it algebraic sum} is defined by $A
+B~=~\{x=a+b~|~ a \in A \mbox{~~and~~} b \in B \} $ and for
$\lambda \in \ensuremath{\mathbb{R}}$ and $A \in {\mathcal{A}}(X)$
the {\it multiplication} is defined by $\lambda A~=~\{x=\lambda
a~|~ a \in A \}.$

The {\it Minkowski sum}   for $A,B \in {\mathcal{A}}(X)$ is
defined by%
\vspace*{-1.7mm}%
$$A~\dotplus B~=~\mbox{\rm cl}(\{x=a+b~|~ a \in A \mbox{~~and~~}
b \in B \}), $$ where $\mbox{\rm cl}(A)= \bar{A}$ denotes the
closure of $A \subset X$ with respect to $\tau.$\par

For a Hausdorff topological real vector space  $X$ two pairs
$(A,B),(C,D)\in {{\mathcal{B}}^{2}}(X) = {{\mathcal{B}}}(X) \times
{{\mathcal{B}}}(X) $ are called {\it equivalent}  if
$B\stackrel{\cdot}{+} C=A\stackrel{\cdot}{+} D$ holds and $[A,B]$
denotes the equivalence class represented by the pair $(A,B) \in
{{\mathcal{B}}^{2}}(X).$ An ordering  among equivalence classes is
given by  $[A,B] ~\leq ~[C,D]  $  if and only if  $ A ~\dotplus ~
D ~\subset ~ B ~\dotplus ~ C.$ This is the ordering on  the
Minkowski---R{\aa}dstr\"om---H\"ormander space and is independent
of the choice of the representatives. Note that this definition is
a natural  extension  of the  former definition  of
``equivalence'' and ``ordering'' for compact convex subsets of the
dual space $X'$ from a Banach space $X$ endowed with the
weak-$\ast$-topology $\sigma(X,X^{'}).$

For  $A\in {{\mathcal{B}}}(X)$ we denote by ${\rm ext}(A)$ the set
of its extreme points and by exp$(A)$ the set of its exposed
points (see [5]). Next, for $A, B \in {\mathcal{A}}(X)$ we define:
$ A\vee B= \mbox{\rm cl~}\mbox{\rm conv}(A \cup  B), $ where
$\mbox{\rm conv}(A \cup  B)$ denotes the convex hull of $A\cup B.$
 We will use the
abbreviation $A ~\dotplus B \vee  C$ for $A \dotplus (B \vee  C)$
and $C + d$ instead of $C +  \{d\}$ for all bounded closed convex
sets $A,B,C \in {\mathcal{A}}(X)$  and a point $d \in X.$

A distributivity relation between the Minkowski sum and the
maximum operation is expressed by the {\it Pinsker Formula} (see
[9]),  which is stated in a more general form in [5].

\textbf{Proposition 1.} Let $(X, \tau)$ be a Hausdorff topological
vector space, $A,B,C \in {\mathcal{A}}(X)$ and~$C$ be a convex
set. Then
\vspace*{-1.7mm}%
$$(A ~\dotplus C) \vee (B~\dotplus  C) = C
~\dotplus (A \vee B). \eqno{\rm   (PF)}
$$
%
 The following generalization of the  Minkowski---R{\aa}dstr\"om---H\"ormander
 Theorem is due to R. Urba\'nski [11].

\textbf{Theorem 3.}{  \it Let $X$ be a Hausdorff topological
vector space. Then for any $A   \in {\mathcal{A}}(X),$ $B \in
{{\mathcal{B}}^{\ast}}(X)$ and $C \in {\mathcal{C}}(X)$ the
inclusion%
\vspace*{-1.7mm}%
$$ A+B \subseteq C ~\dotplus B  \quad \mbox{\it implies} \quad A \subseteq C. \eqno{({\rm  olc})}$$}%
%
This implies that ${{\mathcal{B}}}(X)$ endowed with the Minkowski
sum ``$\dotplus$''  and the ordering induced by inclusion is a
commutative ordered {\it semigroup} (i.~e. a ordered set endowed
with a group operation, without having inverse elements), which
satisfies the order cancellation law and contains
${{\mathcal{K}}(X)}$ as a sub-semigroup.

In this section we study a generalized separation property for
convex sets.

\textbf{ Definition 2.} {\rm Let $X$ be a topological vector
space, $I$ be a finite index set  and  $S, A_i \in
{{\mathcal{B}}}(X), ~i \in I  .$ We say that the set $S$}
\mathdef{separates} {\rm the sets $A_i, ~i \in I  $, if and only
if $\left(\mbox{\rm conv}\left \{a_i~\mid~i \in I \right\}\right)
\cap S\neq \emptyset$ for every collection  $a_i \in A, ~i \in I .
$ }

\noindent use the notation $\bigvee_{i \in I} \left\{ a_i\right\}$
for $\mbox{\rm conv}\left \{a_i\mid i \in I \right\}$ and write
$\displaystyle{\sum_{i=1}^k  A_i ~=~ A_1\dotplus A_2 \dotplus
\cdots\dotplus A_k}.$\linebreak%
Now we have the following algebraic characterization of the
separation property.

\textbf{Theorem  4.} {\it Let $X$ be a topological vector space,
$I$ be a finite index set  and  $S, A_i \in {{\mathcal{B}}}(X), ~i
\in I  .$ Then $S$ separates the sets $A_i, ~i \in I$, if and only
if $$\sum_{i \in I } A_i~ \subset ~\bigvee_{i \in I}  \bigg{(}
\sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k  \bigg{)} ~\dotplus~ S.$$}

{\em Proof.} {\it Necessity.} Let $a_i\in A_i, ~i \in I$, be
given. Then there exist $\displaystyle{\alpha_i \geq 0,\,\sum_{i
\in I} \alpha_i =1}$ such that $\displaystyle{\sum_{i \in I}
\alpha_i a_i \in S.}$ Therefore,
%
\begin{eqnarray*}
\sum_{i \in I}  a_i  & = & \sum_{i \in I}    \bigg{(} \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} \alpha_k   \bigg{)}   a_i ~\dotplus~\sum_{i \in I} \alpha_i a_i= \\
~&=&  \sum_{i \in I}  \alpha_i   \bigg{(}  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} a_k   \bigg{)}   ~\dotplus~\sum_{i \in I} \alpha_i a_i\in %\\
~ \bigvee_{i \in I}   \bigg{(}  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}}
A_k \bigg{)}  ~ \dotplus~ S,
\end{eqnarray*}
%
which proves the necessity.

{\it Sufficiency.} Now fix any  $a_i\in A_i, ~i \in I.$ Then it
follows from the assumption:
$$\sum_{i \in I } A_i~ \subset ~\bigvee_{i \in I}   \bigg{(} \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k   \bigg{)}  ~ \dotplus~ S$$
that for every $i \in I$
$$ a_i + \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k~ \subset ~\bigvee_{i \in I}   \bigg{(}  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k  \bigg{)}  ~ \dotplus~ S,$$
which means
$$  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k~ \subset ~\bigvee_{i \in I}   \bigg{(}  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k  \bigg{)}  ~ \dotplus~ \left(S -a_i\right),~~i \in I.$$
From the Pinsker  formula we get

\begin{eqnarray*}
 \bigvee_{i \in I}  \bigg{( } \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k  \bigg{) } &~ \subset ~& \bigvee_{i \in I} \bigg{[}\bigvee_{i \in I}   \bigg{( }  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k   \bigg{) }  ~ \dotplus~ \left(S -a_i\right)\bigg{]} =\\
 ~&=~&\bigvee_{i \in I}   \bigg{( }  \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k   \bigg{) }  ~\dotplus~\bigvee_{i \in I} \left(S -a_i\right)
\end{eqnarray*}
and gives by the order cancellation law that $\displaystyle{0 \in
\bigvee_{i \in I} \left(S -a_i\right).}$

Now again by the Pinsker  formula we get $\displaystyle{0 \in
\bigvee_{i \in I} \left(S -a_i\right) ~=~S ~\dotplus~\bigvee_{i
\in I} \left\{ -a_i\right\},}$ which implies that $\left(\mbox{\rm
conv}\left \{a_i~\mid~i \in I \right\}\right) \cap S\neq
\emptyset.$
%
 \hfill\square

shadowing} is also used (see [5,~p.~67   and~77] ) to express the

separation by sets} is as follows: {\it if the sets $A, B, S $ are
considered as celestial and $A$ shines, then $S$ separates $A$ and
$B$ if and only if $B$ lies in the shadow of $S.$} Of special
interest is the separation of two sets $A$ and $B$ by a convex set
$S, $  the case $A,B, S \in {{\mathcal{B}}}(X)$   which is

Then $S$ separates the sets $A, B $ if and only if $$
A+B\subset A\vee B~\dotplus S.          \eqno{\rm (SL)}    $$

The formula (SL) is called the {\it separation law}. Now it turns
out that the separation law  is equivalent to the order
cancellation law, as we will see in the following section.

\textbf{5.   The separation and the order cancellation law. }

\textbf{Theorem  5.} {\it Let $A, B$ be  nonempty subsets of a
topological vector space $X$ and assume that $A \vee B \in
{\mathcal{B}}(X).$ Moreover, let $S$ be a  closed convex subset of
$X.$ Then $S$ separates the sets $A$ and $B$ if and only if
$$A+B\subset A\vee B~\dotplus S.$$}
\indent {\em Proof.} {\it Necessity.~} \rm Fix any $a\in A$ and
$b\in B.$ Then it follows from the assumption $A+B\subset A\vee
B~\dotplus S$ that $a+B\subset A\vee B~\dotplus S$ and $b+A\subset
A\vee B~\dotplus S.$ Hence $B\subset A\vee B~\dotplus S-a$ and
$A\subset A\vee B~\dotplus S-b.$ By this inclusions and the
Pinsker formula (PF) we have $A\vee B\subset A\vee B~\dotplus
(S-a)\vee (S-b)=A\vee B~\dotplus (S+[-a,-b]).$ Now we obtain from
the order cancellation law { (olc)} that $0\in S+[-a,-b].$ Hence
$[a,b]\cap S\not=\emptyset.$

{\it Sufficiency.} \rm Now assume that formula (SL) is satisfied.
Let $A, B, C\in {\mathcal{A}}(X)$ and assume that $B$ is bounded
and $C$ is closed and convex. Now take any $a\in A $ and $b\in B.$
From $A+B\subset B~\dotplus C$ it follows that $a+(B-b)\subset
(B-b)~\dotplus C.$ Therefore, $\{0\}+(B-b)\subset (B-b)\vee
\{0\}~\dotplus (C-a).$ Now from the separation law (SL) it follows
that the set $C-a$ separates the sets $\{0\}$ and $B-b.$ Hence
$0=\alpha 0+\beta 0\in C-a$ for some $\alpha,\,\beta \geq
0,\,\,\alpha+ \beta=1.$ So, we have $0\in C-a.$ Hence $A\subset
C.$ \hfill \square

\textbf{ 6. The Demyanov difference.} Demyanov original
subtraction  $A\ddot{-}B$ (see [12]) of compact convex subsets in
finite dimensional space is defined with the help of the Clarke
subdifferential  (see [13]) of the difference of support
functions, i.~e.
$$ A\ddot{-}B =
{\partial_{\rm cl} (p_A~-~p_B ) \Big{|}_{0}},$$ where $p_A$ and
$p_B$ are the support functions of $A$ and $B,$ i.~e.
$\displaystyle{ p_{A}(x) = \max_{a \in A}~\langle a,x \rangle}. $
This can be equivalently formulated by
$$A\ddot{-}B=\overline{\rm conv}\{a-b|a\in A, b\in B, a+b\in \exp (A+B)\},$$
here $ \exp (A+B)$ are the exposed points of $A+B.$ For the proof
see   } Proposition 2 in~[12] and note that every exposed point of
$A+B$ is the unique sum of an exposed point of $A$ with an exposed
point of $B.$

To extend the definition of the difference $A\ddot{-}B$ to locally
convex topological vector spaces, the set of exposed points will
be replaced by the set of extremal points of $A+B.$ This leads to
the following generalization of the
  Demyanov difference.

\textbf{Definition 3.} Let $(X, \tau) $ be a locally convex vector
space and $\mathcal{K}(X)$  the family of all nonempty compact
convex subsets of $X$. Then for $A,B\in\mathcal{K}(X)$ the set
$$A\ddot{-}B=\overline{\rm conv}\{a-b|a\in A, b\in B, a+b\in {\rm ext} (A+B)\} \in\mathcal{K}(X)$$
is called the {\it Demyanov difference} of  $A$ and $B.$

This is a canonical generalization of the above definition,
because  for every $A,B\in\mathcal{K}(X)$ every extremal point $z
\in {\rm ext} (A+B)$ has a unique decomposition $z = x +y$ into
the sum of two extreme points $x  \in {\rm ext} (A)$ and $y  \in
{\rm ext} (B)$ (see [14, Proposition 1]).
 Since in the finite dimensional case the exposed points are dense in the set of extreme points of a compact convex set, this definition
coincides with the original definition of the Demyanov difference
in finite dimensional spaces. Note that in [4] a generalization of
the Demyanov difference to arbitrary topological vector spaces is
also given.

The Demyanov difference  possesses many important properties and
in [15]  the following statement is proved.

\textbf{Proposition 2.} Let $X$ be a locally convex vector space
and $ A,B,C \in\mathcal{K}(X)$.  The Demyanov difference has the
following properties:

{\rm (D1)}.\ \  If $A=B+C$, then $C=A\ddot{-} B$.%\\

{\rm (D2)}.\ \  $(A\ddot{-}B) + B\supseteq A$.%\\

{\rm (D3)}.\ \ If $ B \subseteq A,$  then  $ 0 \in A \ddot{-} B$.%\\

{\rm (D4)}.\ \  $(A\ddot{-}B) = -  (B\ddot{-}A)$.%\\

{\rm (D5)}.\ \ $A\ddot{-} C\subset (A\ddot{-} B)+(B \ddot{-}C).$

%
%
\textbf{7. Demyanov difference and separating sets.} From property
(D2) of the above proposition a theohem follows immediately.

\textbf{Theorem 6.} {\it Let $X$ be a locally convex vector space,
$I$ be a finite index set  and  $S$, $A_i \in \mathcal{K}(X), ~i
\in I .$ Then the Demyanov difference
  $$S~=~\bigg{(} \sum_{i \in I } A_i\bigg{)}  ~\ddot{-}~ \bigvee_{i \in I}  \bigg{(} \sum_{ k \in I \setminus \{i\}} A_k  \bigg{)} $$
  separates the sets $A_i, ~i \in I.$

  }

\textbf{Corollary.} Let  $ A_1,  A_2,..., A_k \in
\mathcal{K}({\mathbb{R}^n})$ be given. Then for the Demyanov
difference holds
  $$\bigg{(} \sum_{i =1 }^k A_i\bigg{)}  ~\ddot{-}~ \bigvee_{i=1}^k \bigg{(} \sum_{ j=1\atop j \not= i}^k A_j  \bigg{)} ~=~ \partial_{\rm cl} P {\big{|}}_{0},$$
where $\displaystyle{ \partial_{\rm cl} P \big{| }_{0}}$  is the
Clarke subdifferential of  $\displaystyle{P = \min\left\{  p_{A_1}
, p_{A_1}  , ...,p_{A_k}  \right \}}$ at $0 \in \mathbb{R}^n,$
i.~e. the minimum of the support functions of the sets  $A_i.$

 {\em Proof.~} This follows immediately from the definition of the Demyanov difference for the finite dimensional case  (see [12]) and the formula
 $$ \displaystyle{\big{(} \sum_{i =1 }^k  p_{A_1}\big{)}~ - ~\max \left \{   \sum_{ j=1\atop j \not= i}^k   p_{A_1} ~|~ i   \in \{1,..,k\}~   \right\}   = \min\left\{  p_{A_1}  , p_{A_1}  , ...,p_{A_k}  \right \}},$$
 which completes the proof.
